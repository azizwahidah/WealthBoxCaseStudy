An AI Financial Advisor for the Rest of Us

Wealthbox AI, or, the questions the existing tools don't know how to ask.

A licensed financial planner, three years inside wealth management, and a first-generation woman of color walks into a build. This is what came out, and what I learned.

§ 01 THE GAP

Real financial advice is still reserved for the wealthy.

A million in assets gets you a human planner who reviews every document with a dollar sign on it. Tax, estate, insurance, cash flow, the weird stuff that never fits a spreadsheet. Everyone else gets, "When do you want to retire? Great, here's your ETF."

That isn't advice. That's a fund pick.

I spent three years inside wealth management firms, managing 80+ HNW households at Brio Financial Group and supporting $3M+ households at Litman Gregory. I watched, up close, what real financial planning looks like when it's resourced. Then I'd go home to a community where the same questions had no good answers, because the people asking them weren't profitable enough to serve.

The robo-advisor wave was supposed to fix this. It didn't. It made cheap portfolio allocation widely available, then declared the problem solved. The actual problem, what the wealthy pay planners to handle, never got automated. It just got ignored for everyone outside the AUM minimums.

AI changes the math. For the first time, the cost of giving real planning to someone with $5,000 isn't structurally different from the cost of giving it to someone with $5,000,000. The question is who builds it, and what they assume about the customer.
§ 02 THE QUESTIONS

Most tools assume a nuclear family and a linear accumulation path. Most of my audience has neither.

The first-generation wealth builder, the one out-earning her parents, the high achiever quietly funding everyone else's life, has financial obligations that go up, down, and sideways at the same time. Existing AI advisors don't know how to hold that. They were trained on a customer who doesn't exist for most of us.

So I started by writing down the questions the existing tools don't know how to ask:

  1. How do you save for your parents' retirement while saving for your own?
  2. How do you fund a niece's tuition, a sibling's wedding, a cousin's visa fees, while still hitting your 401k match?
  3. How do you balance building wealth for yourself, your parents, and your future kids at the same time?
  4. And the one nobody talks about: money solves a lot of problems. It doesn't solve scarcity. After years of only saving, how do you actually learn to spend?

These aren't edge cases. They are the median case for a huge share of the customers fintech now claims to want to reach. They're also the reason a robo-advisor that picks ETFs and calls it advice will keep failing this segment.

§ 03 THE BUILD

I'd never written a line of code. I built it anyway.

Wealthbox AI is a working AI financial advisor designed to ask the questions above before it answers anything. It treats family obligations as a first-class input, not an afterthought. It distinguishes between the math problem (cash flow, allocation, tax) and the harder problem (the scarcity mindset that no spreadsheet resolves).

I made specific product choices that fall out of lived experience and licensed training, not vibes:

The intake doesn't start with retirement. It starts with who else depends on you. For most of my audience, that's the actual gating constraint on every other goal. Tools that ask retirement first reveal whose customer they were designed for.

It distinguishes between what's optional and what isn't. "Sending money home" isn't a discretionary line item to be optimized away. Treating it as one is how robo-advisors lose trust in the first 90 seconds. Wealthbox treats obligations as inputs to plan around, not problems to fix.

It names the spending problem. When a high-saver's plan is mathematically fine but psychologically locked, the tool says so. Most advisors won't, because there's no fee event in resolving it. There should be.

You can try the demo here: wealth-box-ai.vercel.app (it's a working prototype, not a licensed advice tool. Treat it as a demo of how the build thinks, not as financial advice.)

§ 04 WHAT I GOT WRONG

Two things, honestly.

The model gives generic advice until a planner forces it not to. My early versions would happily answer "should I pay down debt or invest?" with a balanced both-sides response, the kind of answer that sounds reasonable and helps no one. A real planner doesn't do that. A real planner runs a proper order of operations (emergency fund, employer match, high-interest debt, tax-advantaged accounts, then taxable, in that order, with exceptions for specific situations) and asks follow-ups before offering anything that looks like a recommendation. I had to teach the model to do that. I had to give it strict parameters on the order of importance, the suitability questions it needs to ask before responding, and the compliance rails it can't step outside of. None of that came out of the box. All of it came from training a CFP-exam-passed, Series 65 licensed planner spent years getting.

I underestimated how much my own training was doing in the background. When I tested the tool with people outside finance, I realized I'd embedded planner instincts (how to sequence questions, when to push back, when to let a number sit) that didn't transfer automatically to the model's behavior. I had to make those instincts explicit. Which is, honestly, a useful exercise nobody at any of my previous firms had ever asked me to do. It's also the exact kind of work a team building an AI financial product needs a licensed planner to lead, not consult on at the end.

§ 05 WHAT THIS IS FOR

Wealthbox is a proof, not a company. The proof is the point.

I'm not raising. I'm not pitching this as the next big consumer fintech. Wealthbox exists because the fastest way to be useful in the rooms where AI financial products are getting built is to have actually built one, made the choices, hit the walls, and learned what's harder than it looks.

If your team is building in this space, the product I shipped is less interesting than the decisions behind it. That's what I want to bring into a room.

If someone is going to build an AI financial advisor for the rest of us, it should come from someone who lived it.
